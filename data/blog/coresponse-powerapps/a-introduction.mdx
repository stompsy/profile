## History

We started a _Community Paramedic_ division at our fire department. It is a relatively new concept that helps to reduce community reliance on Emergency Services (police, fire, EMS) and Hospital Emergency Department resources.

In the 70s, President Nixon urged fire prevention. It has had a huge impact on building codes (sprinkler systems, fire breaks, etc.) and public education that starts with our school-age children as early as kindergarden.

Fire departments traditionally _respond_ to a fire or EMS alarm, _reacting_ to an emergency. We determine if the patient is "sick" or "not sick" and then manage the emergency appropriately, returning to service to prepare for the next emergency as quickly as possible. This type of response and return to service is necessary to keep resources available in the event of a true emergency; heart attack, stroke, vehicle accident, fire, etc.

_Community Paramedic_ programs were implemented, using a similar model of prevention, to reduce preventable health-related emergencies by augmenting the local healthcare system (hospitals, clinics, EMS) and addressing chronic and/or complex medical conditions in vulnerable populations (i.e. elderly, homebound, homeless, substance use disorder, mental health problems).

## Problem

Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) - ..."generally prohibits healthcare providers and businesses called covered entities from disclosing protected information to anyone other than a patient and the patient's authorized representatives without their consent." (Wikipedia article [Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_Insurance_Portability_and_Accountability_Act))

- When we began the _Community Paramedic_ program, we were using traditional Electronic Health Record (EHR) software to record fire, accicent, medical care during an incident. We quickly realized that this was cumbersome when much of what we were doing was providing patients with connections to services. With the exception of _treat-and-release_ medical procedures (i.e. blood draw, suture removal, wound management, medication adminstration by injection or infusion, etc.), we often simply provide medical advocacy for people. This can be in the form of thoroughly evaluating a patient prior to a doctor's visit (i.e. medication reconciliation, obtain vital signs, EKG, blood draw for lab work), then attending the appointment and helping the physician develop an appropriate care plan.

- We often found that a simpler form of documentation was needed. Documenting a phone call or doctor's visit shouldn't require 15-30 minutes of writing a very detailed patient report.
- Our city IT infrastructure does not include a public facing portal that allows for submitting referrals in a HIPAA-compliant manner.
- Our city IT department does not have an abundance of personnel to create and manage our own patient tracking software.
- Our solution needs to be portable so we can access patient records and document patient care and procedures in the field.
- Mobile integrated health software is very expensive. If we're currently spending $100,000 per year on EHR software, we cannot afford to spend another $50,000 per year on separate MIH software for a few Community Paramedics.
- We need to be able to generate reports for grant reporting reqirements.
- Need to be able to generate reports in the event law enforcement or a patient need records for court cases, etc.

## Solution

I had researched many possibilities:

- Use my experience as a web developer and write it in React/Node and a 3rd-party service for user, key management, data storage, etc. While not at all a terrible idea and a relatively cheap solution, I also have to work as a Paramedic and they're not paying me to be a web developer. Our IT department was very clear when they said, "If you do that we will not be able to offer any support whatsoever. Our city's legal department will have to ensure it meets HIPAA requirements. Good luck, buddy."
- Use a managed, HIPAA-compliant SAAS: expensive.
- Use _Epic_ software. This would not entirely meet our needs. Also is extremely expensive. The furthest we could get with this was get read-only access to Epic EHR software through a local community clinic. No ability to record chart notes.
- Hire a consultant to develop a HIPAA-compliant application. Turns out this isn't super cheap. Then we'd still have to self-host the software and data, and I forgot to add earlier... IT department said, "Haha. Nope."

So, our city IT department directed me to the one available solution...

I had to teach myself how to use MS Power Apps and SharePoint and create and implement an application that would work for us.

It's not the best solution, but it has worked for us very well over the last 3 years.

## We call it _Coresponse_

It allows our fire department and external partner personnel (that work in our office) to refer patients to us at any time during our shift. It sends a notification to us that a referral was generated. We then assign the referral to the appropriate resource; Community Paramedic, case manager, social worker, etc. When we assign personnel to the referral it generates an email that notifies the referrer that someone is assigned to it and supplies contact information. When the referral is closed, it notifies the persons involved that they can contact the Community Paramedic division for details.

It tracks very detailed information about our patients (their primary care providers, insurance, etc.), the referrals connected to them, and every encounter connected to each referral. We can attach documents, pictures, etc.
